Green chemistry in medicinal chemistry

Green chemistry in medicinal chemistry refers to the application of green chemistry principles—originally articulated by Anastas and Warner in 1998[1]—to the design, synthesis, and manufacture of biologically active molecules and pharmaceutical compounds. The central aim is to reduce waste, minimize the use of hazardous reagents and solvents, improve atom efficiency, and lower overall environmental impact while maintaining or improving the yield and quality of drug candidates and active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs).[2]

Medicinal chemistry is recognized as a particularly high-impact area for green chemistry reform because pharmaceutical synthesis historically generates large amounts of solvent waste, relies on stoichiometric reagents with poor atom economy, and produces significant quantities of toxic by-products relative to the mass of product obtained.[3] Quantitative metrics such as atom economy (AE), the E-factor, and process mass intensity (PMI) have been developed to measure and compare the environmental footprint of synthetic routes.[1]

Key strategies employed within this field include the use of catalysis and biocatalysis, alternative and renewable solvents, metal-free functionalization reactions, multicomponent reactions, and continuous processing. A widely cited example is the enzymatic production of 6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA), a key intermediate in semisynthetic beta-lactam antibiotics, using immobilized penicillin G acylase—a route that replaces earlier solvent-intensive chemical hydrolysis with an aqueous, enzyme-catalyzed process.[7]

Despite considerable progress, the field faces ongoing challenges. Green metrics do not always capture full lifecycle toxicity or energy demands, and approaches that work at laboratory discovery scale may not be readily transferable to industrial manufacturing scale. Critical evaluation of claimed environmental benefits remains essential.[3]

1  Background

1.1  Definition of green chemistry

Green chemistry, also called sustainable chemistry, is a design philosophy that seeks to eliminate or reduce the generation of hazardous substances in the design, manufacture, and application of chemical products and processes.[5] It was formally introduced by Paul Anastas and John Warner in their 1998 monograph, which systematized the field around twelve guiding principles. Unlike pollution control, which deals with waste after it is already formed, green chemistry targets the source: the molecular design of reactions and processes.[4]

The approach is not limited to environmental stewardship. Economic and safety benefits often coincide: using less solvent reduces raw-material costs; fewer toxic reagents lower exposure risks; higher atom efficiency means less material is discarded as waste.[1]

1.2  The 12 principles

The twelve principles of green chemistry, as listed by the American Chemical Society, include: (1) waste prevention; (2) atom economy; (3) less hazardous chemical syntheses; (4) designing safer chemicals; (5) safer solvents and auxiliaries; (6) design for energy efficiency; (7) use of renewable feedstocks; (8) reduction of derivatives; (9) catalysis; (10) design for degradation; (11) real-time analysis for pollution prevention; and (12) inherently safer chemistry for accident prevention.[5]

In practice, pharmaceutical chemists most frequently cite principles 1–3, 5, 9, and 10 as directly relevant to synthetic route design.[3] Not all twelve principles can be simultaneously maximized in every synthesis; trade-offs between cost, yield, safety, and environmental impact are common and must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis.

1.3  Why medicinal chemistry is a high-impact area

The pharmaceutical industry has been identified as one of the most solvent- and waste-intensive manufacturing sectors in the chemical industry. Studies from the 1990s and 2000s reported E-factors—ratios of waste mass to product mass—on the order of 25–100 kg waste per kg API for fine chemicals, with individual drug syntheses occasionally exceeding 100.[1] By comparison, bulk petrochemicals typically have E-factors below 5.

This disparity reflects several features of pharmaceutical synthesis: multi-step routes with cumulative yield losses; heavy use of organic solvents for reactions, workups, and purification; stoichiometric use of reagents such as protecting groups that are later removed; and the small scale of early drug discovery campaigns, where process optimization is not prioritized. [2] The high number of synthetic steps also amplifies the importance of each individual step's efficiency.

The scale at which active pharmaceutical ingredients are eventually manufactured—sometimes hundreds of tonnes per year for blockbuster drugs—means that even modest improvements in solvent choice or step count can have substantial aggregate environmental and economic consequences.[3]

2  Key concepts and metrics

Several quantitative metrics have been proposed to evaluate and compare the greenness of synthetic routes. Each captures a different aspect of resource efficiency or waste generation, and no single metric provides a complete picture. [1][3]

2.1  Atom economy

Atom economy (AE), introduced by Barry Trost in 1991, is defined as the molecular weight of the desired product divided by the sum of the molecular weights of all reactants, expressed as a percentage:[1]

AE = (MWproduct / ∑MWreactants) × 100%

A reaction with AE = 100% incorporates every atom of every reactant into the product. Addition reactions such as Diels–Alder cycloadditions approach this ideal. In contrast, substitution reactions that generate stoichiometric salt by-products, or reactions that require protecting group installation and removal, have inherently lower AE. Atom economy is a theoretical calculation based on the balanced equation; it does not account for solvents, reagents used in excess, or actual yield, so it should be used alongside other metrics. [4]

2.2  E-factor

The E-factor, introduced by Roger Sheldon, measures the ratio of the total mass of waste generated to the mass of product obtained:[1]

E-factor = mass of waste (kg) / mass of product (kg)

Unlike atom economy, the E-factor is calculated from real experimental data and includes solvents used in reaction workups and purification. A lower E-factor indicates a more efficient, less wasteful process. Importantly, standard E-factor calculations do not always account for water used as solvent (some versions exclude water; others include it), and they do not weight waste by toxicity—equal masses of sodium chloride and a chlorinated solvent are counted identically.[3]

2.3  Process mass intensity

Process mass intensity (PMI) is defined as the total mass of all materials used in a process divided by the mass of the desired product:[3]

PMI = total mass of materials (kg) / mass of product (kg)

PMI = E-factor + 1, because materials include both what ends up in the product and what becomes waste. The ACS Green Chemistry Institute Pharmaceutical Roundtable has adopted PMI as its preferred metric because it can be calculated consistently across organizations and is inclusive of all inputs—solvents, reagents, water, and catalysts.[6] Industry benchmarks suggest a PMI below 100 is a reasonable target for API synthesis, with values below 10 considered very efficient.

2.4  Solvent toxicity and solvent amount

Solvents typically constitute the largest fraction of the total mass of materials used in pharmaceutical synthesis—often 80–90% of the PMI value.[6] Consequently, solvent selection and solvent reduction have an outsized effect on overall process greenness. Solvent guides published by the ACS GCI Pharmaceutical Roundtable and by major pharmaceutical companies classify solvents by their environmental, health, and safety (EHS) profiles.[6] Preferred solvents include water, ethanol, isopropanol, ethyl acetate, and 2-methyltetrahydrofuran (2-MeTHF). Solvents to avoid include dichloromethane, dimethylformamide (DMF), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) in large quantities, and chlorinated aromatic solvents.

It should be noted that solvent preference can conflict with other synthetic objectives: some reactions require polar aprotic solvents for adequate reactivity, and switching to a greener solvent may require re-optimization of reaction conditions. [2]

2.5  Reagent toxicity and by-product formation

Beyond solvents, the selection of reagents affects both hazard and waste. Stoichiometric oxidants such as chromium trioxide or potassium permanganate generate toxic metallic by-products; catalytic alternatives—including TEMPO-mediated oxidations and enzymatic oxidations—can reduce both waste and hazard.[1] Similarly, classic reducing agents such as lithium aluminum hydride or sodium borohydride can in some cases be replaced by asymmetric transfer hydrogenation or biocatalytic reductions, which are amenable to aqueous conditions and generate less reactive waste. [2]

3  Applications in medicinal chemistry

3.1  Catalysis and biocatalysis

Catalysis is explicitly identified in Principle 9 of the twelve principles of green chemistry as preferable to stoichiometric reagents, because a catalyst is regenerated at the end of each catalytic cycle and therefore generates less waste per unit of product. [5] In pharmaceutical synthesis, homogeneous transition-metal catalysis—including palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions such as Suzuki, Heck, and Buchwald–Hartwig couplings—has largely displaced earlier stoichiometric methods for forming C–C and C–N bonds.[2]

Biocatalysis, the use of enzymes or whole cells as catalysts, has attracted particular attention because enzymes are biodegradable, often operate in water at mild temperatures, and can achieve high enantioselectivity without stoichiometric chiral auxiliaries. [1] Advances in protein engineering and directed evolution have expanded the substrate scope of enzymes far beyond their natural substrates. Applications include ketoreductases for asymmetric ketone reduction, transaminases for reductive amination, and acylases for selective hydrolysis—all steps that are common in drug synthesis.[7]

3.2  Water and alternative solvents

Water is the most abundant, cheapest, and most environmentally benign solvent available. Several classes of reactions have been shown to proceed efficiently in aqueous media, including Diels–Alder reactions, certain aldol condensations, and some organocatalytic reactions. [4] However, many organic substrates of pharmaceutical interest have poor water solubility, limiting direct substitution.

Alternative solvents under investigation include: supercritical carbon dioxide (scCO2), which leaves no solvent residue and can be recycled; ionic liquids, which have negligible vapor pressure but raise concerns about aquatic toxicity and biodegradability; bio-derived solvents such as 2-MeTHF (from furfural) and cyrene (from cellulose); and deep eutectic solvents (DES), which are generally low-cost and biodegradable.[1] Each alternative involves trade-offs in cost, availability, reactivity, and safety that require evaluation before adoption.

3.3  Metal-free and late-stage functionalization

A complementary approach to catalysis is to design reactions that avoid transition metals entirely, particularly when metal residues in the final API could pose regulatory and safety issues.[2] Metal-free late-stage functionalization (LSF) refers to reactions that install or modify functional groups directly on an advanced intermediate or even the final drug candidate, bypassing earlier synthetic steps and thereby reducing overall step count, waste, and solvent use. [2]

Examples include photocatalytic C–H functionalization, electrochemical oxidative coupling, and organocatalytic asymmetric transformations. LSF is particularly valuable in drug discovery, where small collections of analogs are needed rapidly: a single late-stage transformation on a shared scaffold can replace multiple separate syntheses. [2]

3.4  One-pot and multicomponent reactions

One-pot reactions combine multiple bond-forming events in a single vessel without isolation of intermediates. Multicomponent reactions (MCRs), such as the Ugi reaction or the Biginelli condensation, incorporate three or more reactants into a single product in a convergent manner.[4] These strategies can substantially reduce solvent use (no intermediate workup), decrease the number of synthetic steps, and improve overall yield. They are particularly suited to rapidly generating diverse sets of drug candidates in discovery campaigns.

Tandem or cascade reactions, in which the product of one catalytic cycle serves directly as the substrate for the next, similarly improve overall process efficiency by avoiding isolation and purification steps. However, designing compatible reaction conditions for multiple sequential transformations in one pot requires careful optimization. [1]

3.5  Continuous processing and process intensification

Continuous flow chemistry, in which reactants are pumped through a reactor rather than processed in discrete batches, can improve heat and mass transfer, allow the safe use of reactive or hazardous intermediates at small instantaneous volumes, and facilitate automation and real-time monitoring.[3] These features can reduce solvent inventory, improve yield consistency, and decrease energy consumption relative to batch processes.

Several pharmaceutical manufacturers have reported the use of continuous processing for hazardous reaction steps—such as diazotizations, nitrations, and hydrogenations—where the small reactor volume reduces risk and waste. Regulatory agencies have increasingly accepted continuous manufacturing for API production, and process intensification through flow chemistry is now considered an important tool in green pharmaceutical process development.[3]

4  Case study: 6-aminopenicillanic acid

6-Aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA) is the bicyclic beta-lactam–thiazolidine core produced by cleaving the phenylacetyl side chain from penicillin G (benzylpenicillin). It is the key synthetic precursor to a broad range of semisynthetic beta-lactam antibiotics, including ampicillin, amoxicillin, and several first-generation cephalosporins, all of which are produced by reacylating 6-APA with different side chains.[7] Annual industrial production of 6-APA is estimated at thousands of tonnes.

4.1  The enzymatic route and its green credentials

The classical chemical method for producing 6-APA required protecting the carboxyl and amino groups of penicillin G before deacylation, followed by deprotection—a sequence that consumed large amounts of anhydrous organic solvents (typically methylene chloride and dimethylaniline) at low temperatures (−40 °C), and generated phosphorylated or silylated by-products. [7]

The enzymatic route uses immobilized penicillin G acylase (PGA; also called penicillin amidase, EC 3.5.1.11), most commonly from Escherichia coli, to hydrolyze the phenylacetyl amide bond selectively under mild aqueous conditions at approximately pH 7.5 and 25–35 °C, with no organic solvent required for the hydrolysis step itself.[7] The enzyme is typically immobilized on an inert support, allowing it to be recovered and reused over many reaction cycles, which reduces both the cost contribution of the biocatalyst and the quantity of biological waste generated.

Compared with the chemical route, the enzymatic process has been reported to use significantly less solvent per kilogram of 6-APA produced and to eliminate the need for cryogenic conditions. It has become the dominant industrial process for 6-APA manufacture.[7]

4.2  A balanced assessment

While the enzymatic route for 6-APA production is widely cited as a successful green chemistry application, a complete assessment requires consideration of factors beyond the reaction step itself. Relevant questions include: what solvents are used in downstream purification and crystallization? What is the energy input for fermentation of the enzyme-producing microorganism? What is the lifetime and disposal path of the immobilized enzyme support? What is the yield relative to available penicillin G feedstock? How are the phenylacetic acid by-product and the aqueous waste streams treated? [3]

These questions illustrate a general principle: demonstrating that a reaction step uses water instead of organic solvent is necessary but not sufficient to conclude that a process is green. Full lifecycle and PMI analyses that cover the entire manufacturing route, including raw material sourcing, energy use, and waste treatment, are required for a comprehensive evaluation. [1]

5  Limitations and critical assessment

The adoption of green chemistry principles in medicinal chemistry has been uneven, and several conceptual and practical limitations deserve acknowledgment.

5.1  Metrics do not capture full impact

Atom economy, E-factor, and PMI are all mass-based metrics: they measure how much material is used or wasted, but they do not inherently reflect the toxicity, persistence, or bioaccumulation potential of the materials involved.[3] One kilogram of a persistent organic pollutant and one kilogram of table salt contribute equally to the E-factor. Incorporating hazard weighting—for instance, through EHS scores or life-cycle assessment (LCA)—can provide a more informative picture but requires more data and more complex analysis.[4]

5.2  High yield is not the same as green

A reaction that proceeds in excellent chemical yield may still be environmentally costly if it requires large solvent volumes, energy-intensive conditions, or stoichiometric toxic reagents. Conversely, a lower-yielding reaction can be greener overall if it avoids hazardous materials and reduces waste. Conflating reaction yield with process greenness is a common source of misleading claims in the primary literature.[1]

5.3  Discovery scale versus manufacturing scale

Discovery-phase medicinal chemists typically work at milligram to gram scale and prioritize speed and diversity of analogs over synthetic efficiency. Green chemistry considerations are often secondary at this stage, and routes are designed with chemistry that is reliable and well-understood, not necessarily optimized.[2] Process chemistry—the discipline that prepares a candidate drug for clinical trials and manufacturing—is where systematic route redesign and optimization for PMI, solvent, and waste usually occur. Expecting discovery chemistry to meet manufacturing-scale green standards imposes unrealistic constraints on early research.

5.4  Practical barriers to adoption

Some green alternatives may be technically superior on environmental metrics but remain costly, difficult to procure, or challenging to scale. Enzymes for biocatalysis require optimization for each new substrate; alternative solvents may require re-validation of reaction conditions; continuous flow equipment requires capital investment and operator training. Regulatory agencies also require extensive process validation data, which can slow the adoption of novel process chemistries even when environmental benefits are clear.[3]

Additionally, a greener synthetic route that gives lower purity or introduces new impurities may not be acceptable if those impurities compromise drug safety or efficacy—a constraint that does not apply to commodity chemical production.[1]
